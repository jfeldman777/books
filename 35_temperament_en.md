# Macro-Observation: Temperament

Hippocrates: 460-370 BCE.

Claudius Galen: 129-216 CE.

We know about the Hippocratic temperaments from the works of Galen. The psychological descriptions apparently belong to Galen.

## Four Temperaments

**Sanguine**

- Cheerful, sociable, active.
- Becomes excited quickly and calms down just as quickly.
- Easily switches between activities.
- Superficial in feelings.
- Inclined toward optimism.

**Choleric**

- Hot-tempered, energetic, decisive.
- Reacts quickly and is prone to strong emotions.
- A natural leader, but may be aggressive.
- Unstable in mood.
- Stubborn and inclined toward dominance.

**Phlegmatic**

- Calm, balanced, slow.
- Restrained in emotions and actions.
- Reliable and stable.
- Does not like change.
- Takes a long time to enter a new task, but works persistently.

**Melancholic**

- Withdrawn, impressionable, anxious.
- Inclined toward deep experiences.
- Sensitive to criticism.
- Has creative thinking.
- Often prone to despondency.

## Appendix

We distinguish an instant state that repeats in a person from typical reactions of a person to typical circumstances, that is, character traits.

The set of instant states plus the list of characteristic traits forms character.

Theophrastus, a student of Aristotle, wrote a book called **Characters**, where he listed not 30 characters, but 30 character traits.

The four temperaments are characters.

These characters are formed from warming-up curves against the background of the educator’s typical reaction to the child’s actions.
